How to use it

Use the webpage, the bookmarklet, or the Chrome extension to generate six SEO-friendly heds and subheds or nine social-media posts tuned for Facebook, X, and LinkedIn.

For headlines, you can specify a target keyword and a tone — straight news, urgent/breaking, analytical, conversational, or authoritative. The tool applies SEO rules automatically: front-loaded keywords, 50–60 character targets, active voice. Formats like listicles or question-framing are only used when the article actually supports them — accuracy beats structure.

For social posts, the tool writes to each platform's conventions: punchy for X, conversational for Facebook, analytical and professional for LinkedIn. Both headline and social generation start with a fact-extraction step, so suggestions stay grounded in what the article actually says rather than drifting into inference or hype.

This tool uses Anthropic's API — a different product from the public Claude.ai chatbot. Under Anthropic's API terms, content sent through the API is not used to train AI models and is not retained after your request is processed. Your unpublished article text is never stored on our server either. Think of it like a fax: the text travels to get processed, the answer comes back, and nothing is kept.

Frequently Asked Questions
  • Is my article safe to paste in before publication?
    Yes. The tool sends your text to Anthropic's API (not to the public Claude.ai product). Anthropic's API does not use inputs for model training and does not store your content after the request completes. Our server also does not log or save article text. The only thing that persists is the output — headlines or social posts — displayed in your browser until you close or refresh the tab.
  • Which version should I use — web, bookmarklet, or extension?
    The Chrome extension is the most convenient if you're working in Athena regularly — it puts the tool inside the editor so you never leave the page. The bookmarklet is a good middle ground: one click grabs the article from Athena and opens Headline Lab with the text pre-filled. The web tool is the fallback — it works anywhere and requires nothing installed, but you have to copy and paste manually.
  • How is this different from just using ChatGPT or Claude.ai?
    Public AI chatbots (ChatGPT, Claude.ai, Gemini, etc.) may use conversations to improve their models, and anything entered is processed on shared infrastructure with looser data handling guarantees. Our tool calls the AI via a private API with different, stricter data terms — specifically designed for business use. It also runs through our own server, so your API key and configuration are never exposed to your browser.
  • Do I have to paste the whole article?
    No — a detailed summary works well too. The tool needs enough context to identify the story's main angle, key people or places, and what readers will learn. A paragraph or two is usually sufficient. The more specific you are, the better the output quality for both headlines and social posts.
  • Should I use these headlines as-is?
    Treat them as a strong first draft, not a finished product. The tool now runs a fact-extraction pass before generating, which keeps suggestions grounded in what the article actually says — but it still doesn't know your publication's style guide, your editor's preferences, or the subtle judgment that makes a headline really sing. Use the suggestions as a starting point; they're good at surfacing keyword opportunities and structural options you might not have considered. The final call is always yours.
  • How does the Chrome extension work inside Athena?
    Once installed, the extension injects a Headline Lab button next to the Title field on every Athena post editing page. The button is grayed out until the Content field has body copy. Once it activates, clicking it opens a slide-in panel on the right side of the screen. You can set a target keyword and tone, then generate six headline + subhed pairs. Clicking any headline pastes it into the Title field; clicking a subhed pastes it into the Subhed field — without leaving the editor or switching tabs.
  • What do the colored badges on headlines mean?
    The character-count badge turns green when a headline falls in the 50–60 character sweet spot that Google typically displays in full. It turns red for headlines over 60 characters (they'll be truncated in search results) and stays yellow for headlines under 50 (still valid, but leaving value on the table). The keyword badge shows which search term the AI targeted in that particular headline.
  • What do the colored badges on social posts mean?
    On X posts, the badge shows the character count against the 280-character limit — green means you're within limit, red means the post is too long to publish as-is and needs trimming. Facebook and LinkedIn don't have hard limits, so their badges just show length for reference.
  • Should I use social posts as-is?
    Same principle as headlines: treat them as a first draft. The tool extracts key facts before writing posts, so suggestions stay grounded in what the article actually says — no embellishment, no superlatives the article doesn't use. That said, it still doesn't know your account's voice or audience. A quick edit to add a specific detail or your own phrasing will usually improve them.
  • Can I generate both headlines and social posts for the same article?
    Yes — the two buttons are fully independent. Run Generate Heds first, review the options, then hit Generate SM Posts whenever you're ready. Or skip headlines entirely and go straight to social. Each run is a fresh API call; results from one don't affect the other.
  • Does it cost anything to use?
    Each request costs a small fraction of a cent in Anthropic API fees (roughly $0.003 for headlines, $0.008 for social posts). At typical newsroom usage this amounts to a few dollars a month — negligible compared to the time savings. There's no per-seat cost or subscription for the tool itself.
  • What if I already have a headline in mind?
    Still worth a quick run. Even experienced editors find it useful to see AI-generated alternatives — sometimes one surfaces a keyword angle or structure they hadn't considered. You can always ignore all six suggestions, but they take about ten seconds to generate and often prompt useful second thoughts.
Tips for Best Headline Results
📋

Paste full body text

The tool reads your full text to extract key facts before writing. A longer paste means more accurate fact extraction, better keyword identification, and suggestions that actually reflect what your story says.

🎯

Set a target keyword

If you've already done keyword research or know what readers are searching, enter it. The tool will prioritize fitting that phrase naturally into at least some options.

🎚️

Match the tone

Breaking news needs urgency; a policy explainer needs authority; a features piece might work conversationally. Selecting the right tone produces more usable suggestions.

🔄

Run it twice

Each generation is independent. If the first batch feels off — wrong angle, wrong tone — hit Generate Heds again. The fact-extraction step re-runs too, so you may get a different set of grounded options.

✂️

Adapt, don't just copy

Hybrid headlines often outperform pure AI output. Take the keyword placement from one suggestion, the structure from another, and add your own editorial voice.

📏

Aim for the green zone

The 50–60 character window is not a hard rule, but headlines in this range are statistically less likely to be truncated in Google Search and news aggregators.

Tips for Best Social Post Results
📐

Watch the X character badge

X posts over 280 characters can't be published as-is. If a badge turns red, trim the post before copying — usually cutting a phrase or dropping a hashtag is enough.

🔀

Use different posts for different days

You get three posts per platform. Spread them out. Each one leads with a different angle, so staggering publication keeps your feed from feeling repetitive.

🔗

Add the URL before posting

The tool writes copy only — it doesn't know your published URL. Paste the article link into each post before scheduling. On X, the URL counts toward your 280-character limit.

🏷️

Customize the hashtags

AI-generated hashtags are a reasonable starting point, but you know which tags your audience actually follows. Swap generic ones for the specific communities Defense One reaches.

💼

LinkedIn rewards specificity

LinkedIn posts get more engagement when they speak to a professional outcome — what does this mean for procurement, policy, or readiness? The AI tries, but you can sharpen it.

🔄

Check the facts, then add color

The tool avoids claims not in the article and won't use superlatives the story doesn't earn. That makes posts safer to publish quickly — but you can still add context, a reaction quote, or a sharper angle that the AI missed.

Quick SEO Reference
"Search engines index headlines within minutes of publication. Getting the keyword right at launch matters more than any later correction." General principle, search optimization practice
Element Best Practice Why It Matters
Length 50–60 characters Google Search typically shows ~60 chars; staying under prevents truncation with an ellipsis, which reduces click-through.
Keyword placement As close to the start as natural Search engines weight words earlier in the headline more heavily. Readers also scan-read left to right and need the hook fast.
Numbers Use digits, not words ("5" not "five") Numerals are visually distinct in a list of search results and signal specificity, which improves click-through rates.
Voice Active preferred Active constructions are shorter and more direct, which helps both SEO and reader comprehension.
Accuracy Headline must reflect article content exactly Clickbait headlines that don't deliver trigger high bounce rates, a negative ranking signal. This tool extracts explicit facts first and requires every headline to trace back to something in the article.
Freshness signals Include year or timeframe for evergreen topics "Best mortgage rates 2025" outperforms "Best mortgage rates" for date-sensitive searches.
